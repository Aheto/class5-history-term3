📘 The 1948 Riots

💡 What Were the 1948 Riots?

The 1948 riots were a series of violent protests, looting, and unrest that spread across the Gold Coast (now Ghana) in late February and early March 1948. These riots were a turning point in Ghana's history because they showed the British colonial government that the people of the Gold Coast were no longer willing to accept unfair treatment.

💭 Think About It: Have you ever seen people come together to protest against something unfair? What do you think makes people reach the point of rioting?

🔥 How the Riots Started

The riots began immediately after the shooting of three unarmed ex-servicemen on 28th February 1948. The ex-servicemen — Sergeant Adjetey, Corporal Attipoe, and Private Odartey Lamptey — were marching peacefully from Accra to Christiansborg Castle in Osu to present a petition to the British Governor about their unpaid pensions and war benefits. When they refused to stop at a police roadblock, the police shot them dead.

When news of the shooting spread, people were filled with anger. They had already been suffering from:

  • High prices of goods after the boycott
  • Shortage of basic items
  • Lack of jobs for returning soldiers
  • Broken promises by the British government
  • Foreign control of the economy

🏚️ The Spread of the Riots

The riots quickly spread from Accra to other towns across the Gold Coast, including Kumasi, Koforidua, and Cape Coast. During the riots:

  • Foreign-owned shops were looted — especially European and Syrian trading stores
  • Property was damaged and burned
  • The police and army struggled to control the crowds
  • A state of emergency and curfew was declared in many towns

The riots lasted for several days and caused serious damage to property and businesses. However, they also showed the British that the people of the Gold Coast were united in their demand for self-government.

⛓️ The Arrest of the Big Six

The British colonial Governor, Sir Gerald Creasy, blamed the leaders of the United Gold Coast Convention (UGCC) for inciting the riots. He ordered the arrest of six key leaders, who became known as "The Big Six". They were detained without trial because the government believed they were behind the unrest.

👥 Who Were the Big Six?

The Big Six were the most important political leaders of the Gold Coast at that time. They were all members of the UGCC, the first major political party in the Gold Coast, formed in 1947. Here are the six leaders:

Dr. Joseph Boakye Danquah Founder of the UGCC
Dr. Kwame Nkrumah Later became first President of Ghana
Dr. Ebenezer Ako-Adjei Recommended Nkrumah to the UGCC
Edward Akufo-Addo Later became Chief Justice and President
William Ofori Atta Prominent statesman and politician
Emmanuel Obetsebi-Lamptey First Chairman of the UGCC

📢 The Protests of 8th March 1948

On 8th March 1948, teachers and students across the Gold Coast organised peaceful protests to demand the release of the Big Six. They believed the leaders were being unfairly detained. However, the colonial government responded harshly — the teachers and students who protested were immediately dismissed from their schools.

This showed how determined the people were to stand together for justice, even when facing punishment.

🌟 Why Were the 1948 Riots Significant?

The 1948 riots changed the course of Ghana's history in several important ways:

  • They forced the British to act: The British government sent the Watson Commission to investigate the causes of the riots.
  • They united the people: For the first time, people from different backgrounds came together to demand independence.
  • They led to a new constitution: The Coussey Committee was set up to draft a new constitution giving Africans more power.
  • They inspired future leaders: The events of 1948 inspired a new generation of nationalists, including Kwame Nkrumah, who later led Ghana to independence on 6th March 1957.
