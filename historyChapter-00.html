📘 Journey to Independence: Early Protest Movements

👤 Who was Joseph Ephraim Casely Hayford?

Joseph Ephraim Casely Hayford (also spelled Casley Hayford) was a brilliant Ghanaian intellectual, politician, and writer who fought for the rights of Africans during colonial rule.

  • Lifespan: He was born in 1866 and died in 1930.
  • Birthplace: He was born in Cape Coast.
  • Early Activism: He was a prominent member of the Aborigines' Rights Protection Society (ARPS). He played a very important role in bringing the traditional chiefs and the ARPS together so they could unite against unjust colonial laws, such as the Land Bill.

🌍 His Role in the NCBWA

Casely Hayford was a visionary leader who believed that Africans across West Africa needed to unite to demand better rights and representation.

  • Formation of the NCBWA: He helped to form the National Congress of British West Africa (NCBWA), which was a major early nationalist protest movement.
  • The 1920 London Delegation: In 1920, Casely Hayford led a delegation to represent the NCBWA in London. He bravely stood before the British government to put forward the NCBWA's demands for more African representation and rights in the government.
  • Addressing the League of Nations: He also addressed the League of Nations, which was the first organisation of world governments that worked for world peace, bringing the struggles of West Africans to the global stage.
  • Outcome of his Demands: Although Casely Hayford was a powerful speaker and leader, he was not successful in getting the British government to grant the NCBWA's demands at that time. However, his efforts laid the foundation for future nationalist movements.

📚 Literary Contributions

Aside from his political activism, Casely Hayford was a pioneering author.

  • He wrote six books about West Africa.
  • His most famous book, Ethiopia Unbound, holds a special place in history because it was the first novel written by an African in the English language.
💭 Thought Experiment: Imagine you are living in Ghana in the 1920s. You are part of a group that wants to present demands to the British government. What three demands would you make for your people? How would you feel traveling all the way to London to present them?
